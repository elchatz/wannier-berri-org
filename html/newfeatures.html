Multi-node parallelization

Old WB could compute only on one node. By means or the Ray module now WB can perform calculaions on as many nodes as needed. To find how to bind several nodes in a Ray cluster see here.

Fermi-sea formulas for many quantities

External vs internal terms

For example, the total Berry curvature of the occupied manifold is evaluated by means of Wannier interpolation as (Wang et al. 2006).

\[ \Omega_\gamma ({\bf k}) = +\epsilon_{\alpha\beta\gamma}{\rm Im\,}\sum_n^{\text{occ}}\sum_l^{\text{unocc}}D_{nl,\alpha} D_{ln,\beta} + {\rm Re\,}\sum_n^{\text{occ}}\overline{\Omega}^{\rm H}_{nn,\gamma} -2\epsilon_{\alpha\beta\gamma}{\rm Re\,}\sum_n^{\text{occ}}\sum_l^{\text{unocc}}D_{nl,\alpha}\overline{A}^{\rm H}_{ln,\beta}\]

The first term ‘internal’, and the rest - ‘external’. This distinction is motivated by the fact that if we employ the tight-binding convention (Convention I, see below) for the Bloch sums, then the external terms vanish for a tight-binding model, where the position matrix elements \(\langle \mathbf{0}i|\mathbf{r} | \mathbf{R}j\rangle = \delta_{ij}\delta_{\mathbf{R},\mathbf{0}} \mathbf{t}_j\) between the basis orbitals are usually assumed diaginal. So, for tight-binding models one can proceed as

system=wberri.System_PythTB(model,berry=False,use_wcc_phase=True)
wberri.integrate(system, grid,
            Efermi=np.linspace(12.,13.,1001),
            smearEf=10, # 10K
            quantities=["ahc"],
            parameters = {'external_terms':False}
            parallel = parallel,
            adpt_num_iter=10,
            fout_name="Fe")

Also, for ab initio Wannier functions it makes sence to separate the evaluation of internal and external terms, for better efficiency. Recall that \(D_{ln,\beta}=\langle l| \partial_k H |r \rangle/(E_n-E_l)\) therefore, the internal terms have a very oscillatory behaviour in k-space in regions where degeneracies or avoided crossings happen near the Fermi level. Therefore, internal terms need a much denser grid to converge. In turn, the external terms are heavier to calculate, but are less oscillating, and hence require a smaller grid.

Tetrahedron method

Fermi-surface properties can be evaluated with higher accuracy and without need for smeared \(\delta\)-function. just add parameters = {'tetra':True}

two conventions for the Bloch sums

We implement both conventions for the Bloch sums, as defined in PythonTB documentation

Basis functions for Bloch states maybe written in one of the two ways:

  • Convention I

\[|\chi_j^\mathbf{k}\rangle = \sum_{\mathbf{R}} e^{i\mathbf{k}\cdot(\mathbf{R}+\mathbf{t}_j)} | \mathbf{R}j \rangle\]

  • Convention II

\[|\chi_j^\mathbf{k}\rangle = \sum_{\mathbf{R}} e^{i\mathbf{k}\cdot \mathbf{R}} | \mathbf{R}j \rangle\]

The default (so far) is Convention II (follwoing the tradition of Wannier90), while Convention I is activated by use_wcc_phase=True in wannierberri.System The two conventions give the same result, but different distribution between internal and external terms (see above) With use_wcc_phase=True the external terms usually become smaller (or even vanish for the tight-binding models), therefore it is a preferred way (however, not implemented for all quantities yet)
